Margin Absorption Capacity (MAC) Framework

Executive Summary for Non-Technical Readers

What is MAC?

The MAC framework is an early-warning system for financial market stress. It answers a simple but critical question:

"If a shock hits markets right now, how well can the financial system absorb it?"

MAC produces a single score between 0 and 1. A high score (near 1.0) means markets have plenty of buffers — shocks are absorbed smoothly. A low score (near 0) means buffers are depleted — even a modest shock could trigger a cascading crisis.

Think of MAC as a structural health check for financial markets, updated in real time, using publicly available data. It does not predict what will go wrong, but tells you how bad it will be when something does.

How It Works — The Seven Pillars

MAC monitors seven independent dimensions of market health. Each pillar asks a plain-language question about a specific type of "buffer":

1. Liquidity

Can markets trade without prices jumping wildly? Measures funding costs and dealer capacity.

2. Valuation

Are investors being paid enough to take risk? Detects both complacency (spreads too tight) and panic (spreads too wide).

3. Positioning

Is leverage manageable? Tracks crowded trades — especially the Treasury basis trade — that cause forced selling in a downturn.

4. Volatility

Is volatility at a healthy level? Too low signals complacency; too high signals crisis. Extended calm periods get penalised.

5. Policy

Can the central bank respond? If rates are already near zero and the balance sheet is large, there's less firepower left.

6. Contagion

Is stress spreading across borders? Monitors dollar funding costs, emerging-market capital flows, global banking health, crypto–equity correlation, and crypto futures leverage (open interest). Rising BTC–S&P correlation signals correlated risk-on positioning across traditional and digital asset classes.

7. Private Credit

Is the $1.7 trillion private credit market hiding problems? Uses publicly traded proxies since direct data is quarterly at best.

Each pillar produces its own 0–1 score. These are combined into the overall MAC score using weights determined by a machine-learning model trained on 41 historical crisis scenarios.

The Traffic-Light Status System

Rather than asking users to interpret a raw score, MAC translates the number into a five-level status that incorporates not just the current level but also whether conditions are improving or deteriorating:

StatusWhat It MeansSuggested Action
COMFORTABLE Buffers are ample (MAC > 0.65). Markets can absorb large shocks. Maintain strategic allocation
CAUTIOUS Buffers are adequate but thinner (MAC 0.50–0.65). Review risk, prepare contingencies
DETERIORATING Buffers are thinning quickly — MAC is in the cautious range but falling. Reduce equity beta, raise cash
STRETCHED Buffers are dangerously low (MAC 0.35–0.50). Modest shocks could cause disruption. Defensive positioning, hedge tails
CRITICAL Buffers are near or at zero (MAC < 0.35). Regime break risk is high. Maximum defence, preserve capital
Regime Break (MAC < 0.20): When the score falls this low, MAC declares that normal forecasting models no longer apply. The framework switches from providing point estimates to flagging that non-linear, cascading dynamics are likely — analogous to a circuit breaker.

What Makes MAC Different?

Versus single indicators (VIX, credit spreads)

Individual stress gauges only measure one dimension. The VIX tells you about equity volatility but nothing about leverage, policy capacity, or private credit. MAC combines all seven dimensions, catching crises that single indicators miss.

Versus other composite indices

Most financial-conditions indices are coincident — they tell you that a crisis is happening. MAC is designed to be a leading indicator, measuring whether the conditions for a crisis exist before the shock arrives.

A critical finding: positioning predicts hedge failure

In every modern crisis where Treasury bonds failed to act as a safe haven (March 2020, September 2022, October 2023), MAC's positioning pillar was already in breach. This 100% correlation means MAC can warn you in advance when your "hedges might not hedge."

The Country Dimension — GRRI

The MAC score measures market-wide stress, but the same market shock hits different countries differently. The Global Risk and Resilience Index (GRRI) adjusts MAC's output based on a country's structural resilience — political stability, economic strength, social cohesion, and environmental exposure.

A resilient country (e.g. Norway) dampens the shock impact; a fragile country (e.g. a politically unstable emerging market) amplifies it. The combined equation is:

Market Impact = Shock × Country Modifier × MAC Multiplier

What About Gold and Oil?

A common question is whether MAC directly tracks commodity prices — gold, oil, and others. The short answer: MAC does not track commodity prices as standalone inputs, and this is by design.

MAC measures the system's capacity to absorb shocks, not the shocks themselves. A $20/barrel oil spike is the shock. MAC tells you how bad the consequences will be.

That said, the effects of commodity price moves are comprehensively captured across multiple pillars:

Commodity EffectWhere MAC Captures It
Oil shock → inflation spike Policy pillar: Core PCE deviation from target; 5y5y TIPS breakeven inflation expectations. An oil-driven inflation surge constrains the Fed's ability to cut rates, reducing policy buffer.
Oil shock → credit stress Valuation pillar: High-yield OAS widens (energy issuers are ~15% of HY index); IG spreads widen on contagion. Both are scored in real time.
Gold surge → safe-haven demand Volatility pillar: VIX typically rises alongside gold safe-haven flows; realised–implied vol gap widens. Liquidity pillar: Flight-to-quality compresses Treasury yields and funding costs.
Commodity-driven EM stress Contagion pillar: EM reserve coverage (Guidotti-Greenspan ratio) deteriorates for commodity-importing nations; cross-currency basis widens. GRRI modifier: Commodity-dependent countries score lower on the economic resilience pillar.
Oil price collapse → energy sector leverage Private Credit pillar: BDC price/NAV discounts widen on energy exposure; leveraged loan ETFs (BKLN/SRLN) decline; SLOOS tightening accelerates to energy-exposed borrowers.
Gold standard constraints (historical) Policy pillar (historical): Gold reserve ratio directly constrains policy scoring in the 1907–1934 backtest period, capping policy buffer at 0.55 under gold standard regimes.

In short, MAC doesn't need a "gold pillar" or "oil pillar" because commodity shocks propagate through the seven existing pillars — inflation constrains policy, credit stress hits valuation, safe-haven flows move volatility, and EM exposure shows up in contagion. Tracking the commodity price itself would be measuring the cause; MAC measures the consequence — which is what determines portfolio impact.

Data and Transparency

MAC is built entirely on publicly available data from official sources — the Federal Reserve (FRED), CFTC, NBER, Shiller/Yale, Bank of England, and others. No proprietary feeds or black-box models are required. The framework is fully documented, reproducible, and backtested across 117 years of market history, spanning world wars, gold standard transitions, Bretton Woods, stagflation, the Great Moderation, the GFC, COVID, and the 2022–24 hiking cycle.
