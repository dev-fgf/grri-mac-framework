The MAC Framework

Questions CEOs and Regulators Ask — and Honest Answers

Martyn Brush, FGF Research  |  February 2026  |  Confidential Draft

15-Second Elevator Pitch
When markets crash, most investors assume their government bond hedges will protect them. Twice in the last five years, those hedges failed catastrophically — portfolios lost money on both sides. The MAC framework is an early-warning system that monitors seven dimensions of market stress and tells you, weeks in advance, whether your hedges are likely to work or break down. It has been validated against 41 crises spanning 117 years.

What Does MAC Actually Measure?

MAC stands for Margin Absorption Capacity. Think of it as a dashboard with seven gauges, each measuring a different kind of financial buffer. When enough buffers are depleted at the same time, the system becomes fragile — and hedges start to fail.

Liquidity Valuation Positioning Volatility Policy Contagion Private Credit

Each gauge runs from 0 (buffer exhausted — danger) to 1 (ample capacity — healthy). The seven scores are combined into one number that gives a plain-English reading:

MAC ScoreStatusWhat It Means
Above 0.65ComfortableBuffers healthy. Hedges expected to work normally.
0.50 – 0.65CautiousSome buffers thinning. Monitor closely.
0.35 – 0.50StretchedMultiple buffers depleted. Hedges at risk.
Below 0.35CriticalSystemic fragility. Hedges likely to fail.

Show Me: Does It Actually Work?

Example 1 — Where MAC Worked

COVID-19 Crash, March 2020

The fastest bear market in history. The S&P 500 fell 34% in just 23 trading days. Crucially, Treasury bond hedges — the main safety net for pension funds and endowments — failed. Bond prices fell alongside stocks because hedge funds were being forced to sell everything to meet margin calls. A traditional 60/40 portfolio lost money on both sides.

What MAC showed:
20 January 2020 — MAC status moved to DETERIORATING. Seven weeks before the crash peak.
9 March 2020 — MAC fell below 0.45. Volatility pillar at 0.01 (near zero). Policy pillar stressed at 0.29 — the Fed had limited room to cut.
23 March 2020 — MAC hit 0.20, its lowest ever recorded score. Valuation at 0.01, volatility at 0.00, policy at 0.29 — three pillars simultaneously breached.

Result: MAC gave seven weeks of advance warning and correctly indicated that Treasury hedges would fail. A portfolio manager acting on the signal in January could have shifted to alternative protections (options, cash, gold) well before the crash.

Example 2 — Where MAC Worked

The 2008 Global Financial Crisis

Lehman Brothers collapsed on 15 September 2008, triggering the worst financial crisis since the Great Depression. Markets froze globally. Unlike COVID, however, Treasury hedges did work in 2008 — flight-to-quality flows pushed bond prices higher. MAC correctly identified the crisis and correctly indicated that hedges would hold.

What MAC showed:
June 2007 — MAC status moved to DETERIORATING, 15 months before the Lehman collapse.
August 2007 — BNP Paribas froze redemptions. MAC fell to 0.34. Liquidity pillar at 0.00 — completely breached.
September 2008 — MAC at 0.35. Liquidity at 0.00, policy at 0.33 — but crucially, the positioning pillar was not breached, meaning forced Treasury selling was not expected.
Key difference from 2020: In 2008, the basis trade that causes hedge failure was not yet a large-scale strategy. MAC correctly distinguished between "crisis" (yes) and "hedge failure" (no).

Result: MAC flagged severe stress over a year in advance and correctly indicated that Treasury hedges would still provide protection — which they did. Portfolios with bonds benefited from flight-to-quality.

Example 3 — Where MAC Did Not Work

September 11, 2001

The terrorist attacks of 9/11 were a bolt from the blue. There was no financial precursor — no deterioration in liquidity, no stressed positioning, no credit market warning. The crisis originated entirely outside the financial system.

What MAC showed:
Before the attack: MAC was in the CAUTIOUS range at 0.44 — somewhat elevated due to lingering effects of the dot-com bust, but no crisis signal.
After the attack: Liquidity dropped to 0.30 and volatility to 0.25 within days. But the warning came after the event, not before.
Lead time: Less than one week. Effectively zero actionable warning.

Result: MAC did not predict this crisis. And it shouldn't have — this was a geopolitical shock with no market-visible build-up. No financial early-warning system could have detected it. This is a design boundary, not a bug: MAC measures the fragility of the financial system, not the probability that an aeroplane will hit a building.

The honest takeaway: MAC detects crises that build up inside the financial system — leverage, illiquidity, crowded positioning, fading policy capacity. It does not and cannot predict sudden exogenous shocks like terrorist attacks, pandemics' arrival dates, or surprise government decisions. It can, however, tell you how resilient the system is when those shocks happen.

Questions CEOs and Regulators Ask

How is this different from the VIX, which I can already see on Bloomberg?

The VIX measures one thing: expected equity volatility over the next 30 days. It is useful but blind to many risks. During the September 2019 repo crisis, the VIX was 15 (perfectly calm) while overnight lending rates spiked to 10% — a genuine liquidity emergency. VIX said nothing. MAC's liquidity pillar would have flagged it.

More importantly, the VIX cannot tell you whether your hedges will work. VIX spiked in both 2008 and 2020 — but hedges worked in 2008 and failed in 2020. MAC distinguishes between the two because it also monitors positioning, leverage, and policy capacity.

What gives you confidence this isn't overfitted to past crises?

Three things:

1. Scale of validation. The framework has been tested against 41 crises across 117 years (1907–2025), using six external databases. This is not a model trained on the last two crashes — it covers the Panic of 1907, the Great Depression, both World Wars, the Oil Crisis, Black Monday, the Asian Crisis, the dot-com bust, the GFC, COVID, and the 2025 tariff shock. Overfitting to that diverse a set of events would be an achievement in itself.

2. Out-of-sample performance. The machine-learning weights were calibrated on 14 modern crises (1998–2025). They were then applied, unchanged, to 27 earlier crises spanning 1907–1997. The framework still achieved a 75% detection rate on data it had never seen.

3. Transparent inputs. Every data point comes from public sources (Federal Reserve, NBER, Shiller, Bank of England). There are no proprietary black-box signals. Anyone can reproduce the result.

How much warning time do we actually get?

Typically 3 to 12 weeks before the crisis peak. The COVID crash gave 7 weeks of warning. The GFC gave over a year (the first DETERIORATING signal appeared in June 2007 — Lehman didn't fail until September 2008). The UK pension crisis in 2022 gave 12 weeks.

The exception is exogenous shocks like 9/11 or the initial pandemic announcement — events with no financial precursor. For those, MAC gives near-zero warning, but it immediately tells you how fragile the system is once the shock hits.

What does "positioning breach" mean in plain English?

It means too many large investors are making the same bet in the same direction, often with borrowed money. When those bets start losing, everyone rushes for the exit at the same time — and the very act of selling drives prices down further, which triggers more selling.

The specific bet that matters most for Treasury hedges is the "basis trade": hedge funds borrow money to exploit tiny price differences between Treasury bonds and Treasury futures. It's low-risk in normal times, but when margins are called, they're forced to sell bonds into a falling market. That's exactly what broke Treasury hedges in March 2020 and April 2025.

When MAC's positioning pillar drops below 0.20, it signals that this crowding has reached dangerous levels. In our data, every time positioning breached during a crisis, Treasury hedges failed.

What about private credit and shadow banking — does this cover that?

Yes. One of the seven pillars is dedicated to private credit — the fast-growing market of non-bank lending (direct lending funds, business development companies, private debt funds). This market has grown from under $500 billion to over $1.7 trillion in a decade, and much of it is opaque.

The private credit pillar monitors lending standards (via the Fed's Senior Loan Officer Survey), distress signals (BDC discounts to net asset value), and overall leverage. It is the least well-supplied with historical data — the market barely existed before 2004 — but it is precisely the kind of emerging risk that regulators are right to worry about.

Does this work for countries outside the US?

The core MAC pillars are currently calibrated for global markets with a US focus (because the deepest data is US-sourced). However, the framework includes a companion module called the GRRI (Global Risk and Resilience Index), which scores individual countries across four dimensions: political stability, economic robustness, social cohesion, and environmental exposure.

The GRRI acts as a multiplier: for resilient countries (strong institutions, stable governance), shocks are dampened; for fragile countries, shocks are amplified. This country-level layer covers 202 countries and has been extended back to 1789 using academic datasets from the Varieties of Democracy project, the Maddison economic database, and conflict data from Uppsala University.

What about gold, oil, and commodity markets — are they in the model?

MAC does not track commodity prices directly. It doesn't need to. Commodity shocks matter because of what they do to the financial system — and MAC measures those consequences across all seven pillars:

When oil prices spike, inflation expectations rise — that shows up in the Policy pillar (less room for central banks to cut rates). When commodity markets crash, energy companies' bonds get downgraded — that shows up in Valuation (credit spreads widen). When gold surges, it signals fear — that shows up in Volatility and Liquidity. When commodity shocks hit emerging-market exporters, capital flight shows up in Contagion.

The framework deliberately measures the system's capacity to absorb shocks, not the shocks themselves. This is what makes it robust: it works regardless of what caused the stress.

Crypto? Does it matter?

Yes, and it's captured. The Contagion pillar includes Bitcoin–equity correlation (BTC–SPY) and crypto futures open interest as indicators. When crypto becomes highly correlated with equities — as it did in 2022 — it stops being a diversifier and becomes another source of contagion.

What's the false alarm rate? I don't want to be told to panic every month.

The system spends roughly 45% of the time in "Comfortable" (green) and 30% in "Cautious" (amber). "Stretched" or worse occurs about 20% of the time. "Critical" — the strongest signal — occurs in only about 2% of weekly observations, and when it does, something serious is genuinely happening.

Of the five events the framework flagged that didn't result in full-blown crises, all involved extraordinary central bank intervention (LTCM bailout, BNP Paribas, Lehman backstop). The stress was real — the central banks just intervened before it cascaded. That's a feature, not a false alarm: you want to know when the system is fragile, even if someone else manages to prevent the break.

How should a board use this in practice?

Three ways:

1. Standing risk monitoring. Report the MAC score and traffic-light status at every board risk meeting. When the status is green, the report is one line. When it moves to amber, a brief memo explaining which pillars are deteriorating. Red triggers a detailed briefing.

2. Hedge confidence assessment. The key actionable output is whether Treasury hedges — the backbone of most institutional portfolios — are likely to work or fail. When MAC is below 0.40 and positioning is breached, switch to alternative protections: put options, gold, cash, or reduced equity exposure.

3. Scenario planning. The framework includes a shock propagation model that simulates how an initial stress (e.g., a liquidity freeze) cascades through other pillars over time. This provides structured what-if analysis for stress testing.

What can't this system do? Where are the limits?

We want to be direct about the boundaries:

It does not predict when crises will start. MAC tells you how fragile the system is, not when the next shock will arrive. A car's fuel gauge tells you how far you can drive — not when you'll have an accident.

It does not capture exogenous shocks. Terrorist attacks, pandemics, surprise elections, natural disasters — these originate outside the financial system. MAC tells you how well-prepared the system is to absorb them, not whether they'll happen.

Historical data gets thinner as you go back. Pre-1990 results rely on proxy data (1920s credit spreads from Moody's railroad bonds, 1800s volatility from academic reconstructions). These are well-documented and peer-reviewed, but inherently less precise than modern electronic data.

The private credit pillar is young. Non-bank lending as a systemic force is a post-2010 phenomenon. The data for this pillar is shorter than the others, and this is where the next crisis may be least visible.

Central bank intervention can override all signals. When the Fed steps in with unlimited liquidity, market fragility may not produce the expected outcomes. MAC correctly identifies the vulnerability — but it cannot predict whether or when governments will intervene.

In Summary

MAC is not a crystal ball. It is a structural health monitor for the financial system. It measures the capacity of markets to absorb stress — and when that capacity is depleted, it provides weeks of advance warning before the consequences hit portfolios.

Its most important practical output is a simple binary: are your bond hedges going to work, or not? Getting that question right — even once — can save a pension fund hundreds of millions.

The data is public. The methodology is documented. The code is auditable. And we've told you where it doesn't work, not just where it does.
